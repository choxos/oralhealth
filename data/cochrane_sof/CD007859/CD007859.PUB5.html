⊕⊕⊝⊝
Lowa,b

A faster alignment rate represents a benefit for patients.

Results of most studies and most subgroups (different jaws (lower or upper), different segments (3‐3 or 6‐6) and different time points of follow‐up) were consistent, indicating little or no difference in alignment rate between superelastic and thermoelastic NiTi wires (Atik 2019; Azizi 2021; Jain 2021; Keerthana 2021; Mahmoudzadeh 2018; Nabbat 2020; Ong 2011; Phermsang‐Ngarm 2018; Quintão 2005; Ren 2016).

—

52 participants, 312 teeth, 2 studies

⊕⊝⊝⊝
Very lowa,b,c

Less root resorption represents a benefit for patients.

Nabbat 2020 (not included in meta‐analysis) reported a statistically significant difference in root resorption score from before to after treatment in the superelastic NiTi wire group (P = 0.008), but not in the thermoelastic NiTi group (P = 0.083).

Pain
Assessed with: 100‐mm VAS (day 1)
Follow‐up: 7 days

Mean intensity of pain (day 1) in thermoelastic NiTi groups ranged from
29.9 mm to 39.0 mm

—

94 participants, 138 arches, 3 studies

⊕⊕⊕⊝
Moderateb

A higher intensity of pain represents a harm for patients.

Superelastic NiTi probably results in a slight increase in intensity of pain after 1 day and 7 days. Results of other subgroups of different time points showed little or no difference in intensity of pain between superelastic and thermoelastic NiTi wires.

Marković 2015 and Nabbat 2020 (not included in meta‐analysis) reported no statistically significant difference of intensity of pain between superelastic and thermoelastic NiTi groups, while Mahmoudzadeh 2018 (not included in meta‐analysis) reported lower intensity of pain in superelastic NiTi groups than that in thermoelastic NiTi groups. There was little or no difference between the superelastic NiTi group and the thermoelastic NiTi group in analgesic consumption within 7 days (RR 0.77, 95% CI 0.44 to 1.36; Mahmoudzadeh 2018) and until next appointment (RR 1.44, 95% CI 0.98 to 2.10; Azizi 2021; Marković 2015).
